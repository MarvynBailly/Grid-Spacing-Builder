iv. derivationFrom properties to formula

Desired properties

We seek a smooth function \( f:(a,\infty)\to\mathbb{R} \), parametrised by \( a<1 \), satisfying:

  1. a vertical asymptote at the left boundary, \( \displaystyle\lim_{x\to a^{+}} f(x) = +\infty \);
  2. quadratic far-field growth, \( f(x) = \beta x^{2} + O(x) \) as \( x\to\infty \);
  3. a stationary point at \( x=1 \), i.e. \( f'(1)=0 \);
  4. localised influence of the pole: its contribution at \( x=1 \) is exponentially small.

Step 1 — singular term with localised support

Property 1 is realised by a pole of order \( p \), \( g_{0}(x) = \alpha\,(x-a)^{-p} \) with \( \alpha,p>0 \). Algebraic decay alone is too slow to satisfy property 4, so we modulate by an exponential to confine the pole to a thin layer near \( x=a \): \[ g(x) \;=\; \frac{\alpha\, e^{-k(x-a)}}{(x-a)^{p}}. \] The function \( g \) still diverges at \( x=a \) (the polynomial dominates) and decays super-algebraically thereafter; at \( x=1 \) its contribution is suppressed by the factor \( e^{-k(1-a)} \).

Step 2 — quadratic tail

To impose property 2, add \( q(x) = \beta(x-1)^{2} \). Expanding, \[ \beta(x-1)^{2} \;=\; \beta x^{2} - 2\beta x + \beta, \] so the leading coefficient is \( \beta \) as required; the linear and constant pieces are absorbed by the corrections that follow.

Step 3 — linear correction enforces \( f'(1)=0 \)

The candidate \( f(x) = g(x) + \beta(x-1)^{2} + \gamma \) has \( f'(1) = g'(1) \), since \( q'(1)=0 \). Differentiating \( g \), \[ g'(x) \;=\; -\frac{\alpha\, e^{-k(x-a)}}{(x-a)^{p}}\!\left[k + \frac{p}{x-a}\right]. \] Adding a linear term \( -\delta(x-1) \) leaves \( f(1) \) unchanged and shifts the slope at \( x=1 \) by \( -\delta \). Choosing \[ \delta \;=\; g'(1) \;=\; -\frac{\alpha\, e^{-k(1-a)}}{(1-a)^{p}}\!\left[k + \frac{p}{1-a}\right] \] gives \( f'(1)=0 \) by construction. By property 4, \( g''(1) \) is exponentially small, so \( f''(1) \approx 2\beta > 0 \) and the stationary point is a local minimum.

Final form

Collecting the three pieces and the offset, \[ f(x) \;=\; \frac{\alpha\, e^{-k(x-a)}}{(x-a)^{p}} \;+\; \beta(x-1)^{2} \;-\; \delta(x-1) \;+\; \gamma, \] which is \( C^{\infty} \) on \( (a,\infty) \) and satisfies properties 1–4 by construction.

What the parameters do

\( \alpha \) sets the magnitude of the singular term, and \( p \) its order. \( k \) controls the rate at which the pole's influence decays into the domain: larger \( k \) yields a thinner spike near \( x=a \) and a flatter plateau on the rest of \( (a,1) \). \( \beta \) is the leading far-field coefficient, and \( \gamma \) is a vertical offset. The remaining parameter \( \delta \) is not free — it is fixed in closed form by the others through the requirement \( f'(1)=0 \).

v. euler–lagrangeFrom spacing to grid

Variational setup

Given the target spacing \( f(x) \) on \( (a,\infty) \), seek a coordinate map \( x:[0,1]\to(a,\infty) \), \(\xi\mapsto x(\xi)\), that minimises the weighted action \[ S[x] \;=\; \int_{0}^{1} L\bigl(x(\xi),\,x'(\xi)\bigr)\,d\xi, \qquad L \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,f(x)\,(x')^{2}, \] with boundary conditions \( x(0)=x_{L} \) and \( x(1)=x_{R} \). The factor \( f(x) \) penalises large \( |x'| \) where the desired spacing is large, so minimisers naturally bunch grid points where \( f \) is small.

Computing the Euler–Lagrange equation

The conjugate momentum and the gradient of \( L \) are \[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial x'} \;=\; f(x)\,x', \qquad \frac{\partial L}{\partial x} \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,f'(x)\,(x')^{2}. \] Differentiating the momentum along \( \xi \) using the chain rule, \[ \frac{d}{d\xi}\!\left(f(x)\,x'\right) \;=\; f'(x)\,(x')^{2} \;+\; f(x)\,x''. \] The Euler–Lagrange equation \( \tfrac{d}{d\xi}(\partial L/\partial x') - \partial L/\partial x = 0 \) therefore reads \[ \boxed{\;f(x)\,x''(\xi) \;+\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,f'(x)\,\bigl(x'(\xi)\bigr)^{2} \;=\; 0\;}\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad x''+\frac{f'(x)}{2\,f(x)}\,(x')^{2}=0. \]

Gauge invariance: what if \( L = f(x)(x')^{2} - 1 \)?

The new Lagrangian is just \( \tilde L = 2L - 1 \), an affine reparametrisation. Recompute from scratch: \[ \frac{\partial \tilde L}{\partial x'} \;=\; 2\,f(x)\,x', \qquad \frac{\partial \tilde L}{\partial x} \;=\; f'(x)\,(x')^{2}, \] so \[ \frac{d}{d\xi}\!\left(2 f x'\right) - f'(x')^{2} \;=\; 2 f'(x')^{2} + 2 f x'' - f'(x')^{2} \;=\; f'(x')^{2} + 2 f x'' \;=\; 0, \] which after dividing by \( 2f \) is identical to the previous Euler–Lagrange equation. This is no accident: the EL operator is linear, \( \mathrm{EL}[aL+b]=a\,\mathrm{EL}[L] \), so multiplying \( L \) by a nonzero scalar and adding any constant — or, more generally, adding a total derivative \( dF/d\xi \) — leaves the equation of motion unchanged. The \( -1 \) shifts the value of the action by a constant \( -(\xi_{1}-\xi_{0}) \), which the variation does not see.

First integral and equidistribution

Because \( L \) has no explicit \( \xi \)-dependence, the EL equation admits a Beltrami first integral. Multiplying \( f x'' + \tfrac{1}{2} f'(x')^{2}=0 \) by \( x'/\sqrt{f} \) and rearranging gives \( \tfrac{d}{d\xi}\!\bigl(\sqrt{f(x)}\,x'\bigr) = 0 \), so \[ \sqrt{f(x)}\,x'(\xi) \;=\; C \quad\text{(constant)}. \] Separating variables and integrating from \( x_{L} \) to \( x(\xi) \), \[ \int_{x_{L}}^{x(\xi)} \sqrt{f(s)}\;ds \;=\; C\,\xi, \qquad C \;=\; \int_{x_{L}}^{x_{R}} \sqrt{f(s)}\;ds, \] which is the equidistribution principle: the cumulative \( \sqrt{f} \)-arc length of the physical interval is partitioned equally among the \( \xi \)-cells. The constant \( C \) is reported in the side panel.

Numerical inversion

The tick marks on the plot are obtained by computing \( s(x)=\int_{x_{L}}^{x}\!\sqrt{f}\;ds \) once on a fine uniform mesh by cumulative trapezoidal quadrature, then solving \( s(x_{i})=\tfrac{i}{N-1}\,C \) for \( i=0,\dots,N-1 \) by linear interpolation in the inverse direction. Near the pole at \( x=a \) the integrand \( \sqrt{f} \sim \sqrt{\alpha}\,(x-a)^{-p/2} \) is integrable only for \( p<2 \); the implementation guards by starting the mesh at \( x_{L}=a+10^{-3}(x_{\max}-a) \), so \( C \) stays finite for all admissible \( p \).
